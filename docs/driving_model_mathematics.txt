================================================================================
  LANCER Organic AV Training Simulator — Driving Model: Mathematical Foundation
  Vehicle Design Viewport  ·  v9.1 Implementation Reference
================================================================================

This document derives every equation that governs vehicle motion in the
simulation viewport.  The notation follows the source code so that each formula
can be traced directly to an implementation line.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.  COORDINATE SYSTEM AND SIGN CONVENTIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The world is a 2-D top-down plane rendered at 40 px / m.

  Axis      Positive direction
  ────────  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  x_world   East on screen  (screen-right)
  y_world   South on screen (screen-down, i.e. y increases downward)

Vehicle frame  (attached to vehicle, rotates with heading):
  longitudinal  +x_v  =  forward direction of travel
  lateral       +y_v  =  left of the vehicle  (standard SAE convention)

Heading  ψ  (rad):
  Measured clockwise from North (screen-up).
  ψ = 0  →  vehicle faces up  (North).
  ψ = π/2  →  vehicle faces right  (East).

State variables
  v          longitudinal speed  [m/s],  + = forward
  v_y        lateral speed in vehicle frame  [m/s],  + = left
  ψ̇ (r)     yaw rate  [rad/s],  + = counter-clockwise (CCW)
  ψ          heading  [rad],  CW from North
  x, y       world pixel position of vehicle centre

Driver inputs
  δ_ref      reference steer angle  [deg],  + = right
  τ          total applied torque on all drivable wheels  [N·m],  + = forward

Constants (hard-coded in viewport.py)
  m  = 1 500   kg           vehicle mass
  I_z = 2 500  kg·m²        yaw moment of inertia about CG
  C_f = 60 000 N/rad        front cornering stiffness (total, per axle)
  C_r = 60 000 N/rad        rear  cornering stiffness (total, per axle)
  μ  = 0.85                 peak tyre–road friction coefficient
  Δt = 0.016   s            fixed physics timestep  (≈ 60 Hz)
  g  = 9.81    m/s²


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.  VEHICLE GEOMETRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Axle positions are specified as a fraction p ∈ [0, 1] along the frame from
front to rear.  The signed distance from the CG to axle k along the vehicle
longitudinal axis is:

    y_k  =  (p_k − 0.5) · L_frame                                          (1)

where L_frame is the frame length in metres.  Negative y_k means the axle is
ahead of the CG; positive means behind.

For the bicycle model two effective axle distances are computed:

    l_f  =  |mean(y_k) for all steerable axles|                             (2)
    l_r  =  |mean(y_k) for all non-steerable axles|                         (3)
    L    =  l_f + l_r          (effective wheelbase)                         (4)

Special cases:
  • All-wheel-steer (both) with no passive axle: l_f and l_r span front steer
    axle to rear steer axle; L is the distance between the two steer groups.
  • If no steerable axle exists, the frontmost axle is promoted to steerable
    for model stability.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.  LONGITUDINAL DYNAMICS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3.1  Torque distribution
────────────────────────
The user supplies a single total torque τ [N·m].  It is split equally among all
drivable wheels:

    τ_w  =  τ / n_drive                                                      (5)

where n_drive is the total count of drivable wheels (all axles flagged
"drivable", including dual-mounted wheels).

3.2  Longitudinal tyre force per wheel
───────────────────────────────────────
Each driven wheel converts torque to a longitudinal ground force through the
effective rolling radius r:

    F_w  =  τ_w / r                                                          (6)

This is the force demanded at the contact patch.  It does not yet account for
the friction limit.

3.3  Normal load per wheel
──────────────────────────
For longitudinal dynamics, normal load is distributed uniformly across all
wheels (static approximation; load transfer is not modelled longitudinally):

    N_w  =  m · g / n_total                                                  (7)

where n_total is the total wheel count across all axles.

3.4  Tyre width friction scaling
─────────────────────────────────
A wider contact patch supports a proportionally higher friction force.  The
scaling is derived from contact-patch area arguments (area ∝ width × length,
length ∝ N_w):

    w_fac  =  min( 1.5,  sqrt( w_tyre / 0.20 ) )                            (8)

where w_tyre is the tyre width in metres and 0.20 m is the reference width.
The factor is clamped at 1.5, reached at w_tyre ≈ 0.45 m.

3.5  Friction limit and traction saturation (longitudinal skidding)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The maximum longitudinal force that can be delivered without tyre slip is:

    F_lim  =  μ · N_w · w_fac                                                (9)

The actual delivered force per wheel is clamped (saturated):

         ⎧  F_w        if |F_w| ≤ F_lim
    F_act =  ⎨                                                               (10)
         ⎩  sign(F_w) · F_lim     otherwise

When |F_w| > F_lim, the wheel is spinning (drive) or locking (brake), and the
tyre force is limited to the friction cone.  This is the longitudinal skid
condition.

3.6  Speed update
─────────────────
Newton's second law along the longitudinal axis:

    Δv  =  (F_act · n_drive / m) · Δt                                       (11)
    v  ←  v + Δv                                                             (12)

3.7  Rolling resistance
───────────────────────
After the torque step, a first-order drag is applied:

    v  ←  v · (1 − k_drag · Δt)                                             (13)

The drag coefficient k_drag depends on drive state:

         ⎧  0.50   if |τ| < 0.1  N·m   (coasting — brakes + rolling drag)
    k_drag = ⎨                                                               (14)
         ⎩  0.05   otherwise            (driving — only rolling drag)

This is a linear viscous model.  The 0.50 value approximates combined tyre
rolling resistance and mild aerodynamic drag at low speed; the 0.05 value
represents the residual rolling resistance when power overcomes most of the
drag torque.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.  LATERAL DYNAMICS — LINEAR BICYCLE MODEL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

4.1  Model overview
────────────────────
The vehicle is reduced to a single-track (bicycle) model: one equivalent front
wheel and one equivalent rear wheel, each at the centroid of their respective
axle group.  Lateral forces are linear functions of slip angle, saturated at
the friction limit.

4.2  Steer angle distribution
──────────────────────────────
The driver's reference angle δ_ref [deg] (+right) is converted to radians and
distributed by steering mode:

    δ  =  −radians(δ_ref)         (sign flip: model convention + = left)

  Mode        δ_f (front wheel)    δ_r (rear wheel)
  ──────────  ───────────────────  ─────────────────
  "front"     δ                    0
  "rear"      0                    δ
  "both"      δ                    −δ   (counter-steer for tighter ICC)

For "both" mode the rear axle counter-steers, halving the effective wheelbase
and approximately doubling the yaw authority.

4.3  Tyre slip angles
──────────────────────
The slip angle α is the angle between the wheel's heading and the direction of
its velocity.  It is the primary driver of lateral cornering force.

For the front equivalent wheel at distance l_f ahead of the CG:

    α_f  =  sign(v) · δ_f  −  atan2( v_y + ψ̇ · l_f ,  |v| )              (15)

For the rear equivalent wheel at distance l_r behind the CG:

    α_r  =  sign(v) · δ_r  −  atan2( v_y − ψ̇ · l_r ,  |v| )              (16)

The sign(v) factor ensures that the slip angle reverses correctly when the
vehicle moves in reverse, so that cornering forces still act to oppose lateral
slip regardless of direction.

These expressions are evaluated only when |v| ≥ 0.5 m/s.  Below this speed
the lateral dynamics are forced to zero through a fade damper (Section 4.7).

Physical interpretation of each term:
  sign(v) · δ     the wheel's heading offset from the vehicle longitudinal axis
  atan2(…)        the direction of the velocity at the wheel contact point,
                  expressed as an angle in the vehicle frame

4.4  Normal load (static)
──────────────────────────
For lateral dynamics the static load is distributed assuming the CG lies at the
centroid of the effective wheelbase:

    N_f  =  m · g · l_r / L                                                 (17)
    N_r  =  m · g · l_f / L                                                 (18)

This is the quasi-static no-load-transfer approximation.  A heavier rear axle
(l_r small, l_f large) places more load on the front.

4.5  Cornering force — linear model with friction saturation
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The lateral cornering force produced by each equivalent tyre in the linear
regime is:

    F_y,f_linear  =  C_f · α_f                                              (19)
    F_y,r_linear  =  C_r · α_r                                              (20)

C_f and C_r are the total cornering stiffnesses (N/rad) for each axle group
(not per wheel); they subsume all wheels in that group.

Saturation at the friction ellipse (Coulomb limit):

         ⎧  F_y_linear                 if |F_y_linear| ≤ μ · N
    F_y = ⎨                                                                  (21)
         ⎩  sign(F_y_linear) · μ · N   otherwise

When the tyre is saturated, the lateral force is limited to μ · N_axle — the
tyre is in a lateral skid (sideslip / breakaway).

Note: The implementation uses the normal loads N_f and N_r (not per-wheel),
and C_f, C_r are total axle stiffnesses.  This is consistent because the
bicycle model collapses each axle to a single equivalent wheel.

4.6  Equations of motion
─────────────────────────
Applying Newton's second law in the lateral (vehicle-frame y) direction and the
rotational equation about the yaw (z) axis:

Lateral translation:
    m · v̇_y  =  F_y,f + F_y,r  −  m · v · ψ̇                            (22)

Yaw rotation:
    I_z · ψ̈  =  l_f · F_y,f  −  l_r · F_y,r                              (23)

The term  −m · v · ψ̇  in (22) is the centripetal acceleration term: when the
vehicle is rotating, the centripetal force required to maintain circular motion
must be subtracted from the net lateral force to obtain the lateral
acceleration in the vehicle frame.

Discrete-time integration (first-order Euler):
    v_y  ←  v_y  +  [ (F_y,f + F_y,r) / m  −  v · ψ̇ ] · Δt             (24)
    ψ̇   ←  ψ̇   +  [ (l_f · F_y,f − l_r · F_y,r) / I_z ] · Δt           (25)

4.7  Low-speed stabilisation
─────────────────────────────
Below 2 m/s the linear bicycle model becomes numerically stiff because slip
angles diverge as v → 0, and physical lateral damping dominates the dynamics.
An exponential fade is applied:

    fade  =  1 − |v| / 2.0       ∈ [0, 1]  for  |v| < 2 m/s              (26)
    k     =  exp(−fade · 10 · Δt)                                           (27)
    v_y  ←  v_y · k                                                          (28)
    ψ̇   ←  ψ̇  · k                                                           (29)

At v = 0:  fade = 1,  k = exp(−0.16) ≈ 0.852 per tick  →  ψ̇ → 0 quickly.
At v = 2:  fade = 0,  k = 1          →  no damping.

This replaces a full low-speed tyre model (e.g. Pacejka) while keeping the
vehicle controllable at rest.

4.8  Lateral velocity clamp
─────────────────────────────
An additional hard clamp prevents unphysically large lateral velocities at high
speed:

    |v_y|  ≤  |v| · tan(45°)  =  |v|                                       (30)

This limits the sideslip angle β = atan(v_y / v) to ±45°.  Deeper sideslip is
considered a full spin condition beyond the scope of the linear model.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.  UNDERSTEER, OVERSTEER, AND NEUTRAL STEER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

5.1  Steady-state yaw gain and the understeer gradient
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In steady-state circular motion (v̇_y = 0, ψ̈ = 0) the lateral acceleration is:

    a_y  =  v² / R                                                           (31)

where R is the turn radius.  The required steer angle for neutral steer is:

    δ_neutral  =  L / R   [rad]    (small-angle approximation)               (32)

For a real tyre with finite cornering stiffness, each axle requires a slip
angle to generate the necessary lateral force.  In steady state:

    α_f  =  N_f / C_f   at some lateral acceleration a_y                    (33)
    α_r  =  N_r / C_r   at the same a_y                                     (34)

The understeer gradient K_us [rad / (m/s²)] measures how much extra steer is
needed per unit of lateral acceleration:

    K_us  =  m · (l_r / (C_r · L)  −  l_f / (C_f · L))                    (35)
           =  m/L · (l_r/C_r − l_f/C_f)

Substituting the model constants:

    K_us  =  1500 / (L) · ( l_r/60000 − l_f/60000 )
           =  (1500 / 60000L) · (l_r − l_f)
           =  (0.025 / L) · (l_r − l_f)

For the default geometry (l_f = l_r = 1.5 m, L = 3.0 m):

    K_us  =  (0.025 / 3.0) · 0  =  0          →  neutral steer

5.2  Stability conditions
──────────────────────────
  K_us > 0   →   understeering vehicle
    The front tyres saturate before the rears.  At high speed the vehicle
    requires an increasing steer angle to follow a fixed radius.
    The vehicle always turns less sharply than commanded.
    Under friction saturation the front tyre clips (eq. 21) first, and
    yaw rate growth is limited — the vehicle plows straight.

  K_us = 0   →   neutral steer
    Front and rear axle loads and stiffnesses are exactly balanced.

  K_us < 0   →   oversteering vehicle
    The rear tyres saturate before the fronts.  A critical speed exists above
    which the vehicle is directionally unstable (open-loop).

5.3  Critical speed (oversteer)
─────────────────────────────────
For K_us < 0, there is a speed v_crit at which the yaw damping goes to zero:

    v_crit  =  sqrt( −g · L / K_us )                                        (36)

Above v_crit the vehicle diverges in yaw without active corrective steer.
For the default symmetric configuration, K_us = 0 and v_crit = ∞.

5.4  Identifying understeer / oversteer in simulation
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At runtime, the saturation model (eq. 21) provides a natural observable:

  • If F_y,f is clamped and F_y,r is not:  front slides first  →  understeer.
    The vehicle pushes wide (large slip, small yaw response).

  • If F_y,r is clamped and F_y,f is not:  rear slides first  →  oversteer.
    The rear of the vehicle pivots outward (large yaw rate spike).

  • Both clamped simultaneously:  full four-wheel drift.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6.  WORLD POSITION INTEGRATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The vehicle velocity in world frame is a rotation of the body-frame velocity
vector by the heading ψ.  Using the Qt screen convention (y_screen increases
downward):

    ẋ_world  =   v · sin(ψ)  −  v_y · cos(ψ)                               (37)
    ẏ_world  =  −v · cos(ψ)  −  v_y · sin(ψ)                               (38)

The minus sign in (38) arises because the vehicle's forward direction at ψ = 0
points screen-up (decreasing y_screen).

Positions are accumulated in pixel units:

    x_px  ←  x_px  +  ẋ_world · PX_PER_M · Δt                             (39)
    y_px  ←  y_px  +  ẏ_world · PX_PER_M · Δt                             (40)

Heading update (clockwise convention):

    ψ  ←  (ψ − ψ̇ · Δt)  mod  2π                                           (41)

The minus sign in (41): a positive yaw rate (CCW in physics) decreases ψ
(which is measured clockwise).


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7.  ACKERMANN STEERING GEOMETRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

7.1  Motivation
────────────────
For a vehicle to turn without tyre scrub, all four wheels must rotate about the
same Instantaneous Centre of Curvature (ICC).  This requires the inner wheel to
steer at a sharper angle than the outer wheel.  Ackermann geometry provides the
exact angles.

7.2  ICC radius from the reference steer angle
───────────────────────────────────────────────
The driver commands a reference steer angle δ_ref [deg] for the vehicle as a
whole.  The ICC radius from the vehicle centreline is:

    R_ICC  =  L / tan(|δ_ref|)  ·  sign(δ_ref)                             (42)

where L is the effective wheelbase.  R_ICC > 0 for a right turn, R_ICC < 0 for
a left turn.

7.3  Per-axle Ackermann angles
───────────────────────────────
For each steerable axle at longitudinal offset y_axle from the vehicle CG, the
longitudinal distance from the axle to the ICC level is:

    d_i  =  y_ICC − y_axle                                                  (43)

where y_ICC = y_nonsteer_m (the longitudinal position of the non-steerable
axle centroid, which is approximately the ICC level for kinematic steering).

For the rear-steer mode, d_i is forced positive because the steer direction
convention reverses relative to the ICC.

The effective reference angle for this axle is:

    δ_axle  =  atan2(d_i, |R_ICC|)  ·  sign(R_ICC)                        (44)

Note: the `|R_ICC|` in the denominator and the explicit sign multiplication
are critical.  Passing the signed R_ICC directly to atan2 maps left turns into
the second quadrant (≈ 170°) instead of a small negative angle — the source of
a previously corrected rendering bug.

7.4  Inner and outer wheel angles (Ackermann pair)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Given the effective reference angle δ_axle and the track width T:

    R_ref  =  |d_i| / tan(|δ_axle|)    (ICC radius from axle centreline)    (45)

The inner wheel (closer to ICC) must turn sharper than the outer:

    δ_inner  =  atan2(|d_i|,  R_ref − T/2)                                 (46)
    δ_outer  =  atan2(|d_i|,  R_ref + T/2)                                 (47)

Assigning left / right with the correct sign:

  For a right turn (sign > 0):
      δ_right  =  +δ_inner    (right = inner)
      δ_left   =  +δ_outer    (left  = outer)

  For a left turn (sign < 0):
      δ_right  =  −δ_outer    (right = outer)
      δ_left   =  −δ_inner    (left  = inner)

The visual tyre rotation in the viewport uses these per-wheel angles for all
steerable axles, giving geometrically correct Ackermann steering for vehicles
with any number of steer axles.

7.5  Dual-axle steer groups
─────────────────────────────
When multiple axles share a steering group (e.g. tandem steer), each axle
computes its own d_i and therefore its own Ackermann pair.  Axles closer to the
ICC require a sharper turn than axles farther away — the geometry is solved
independently per axle.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8.  DIFFERENTIAL MODEL AND WHEEL ROTATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

8.1  Per-side ground speed during a turn
─────────────────────────────────────────
When the vehicle turns with steer angle δ_ref and track width T:

    R_t  =  L / tan(|δ_ref|)             (turn radius at CG)               (48)

For a right turn (sign = +1) the right side is the inner side:
    v_right  =  v · (R_t − T/2) / R_t   (inner — shorter path)            (49)
    v_left   =  v · (R_t + T/2) / R_t   (outer — longer path)             (50)

For a left turn the assignments flip by the sign.

8.2  Open differential
───────────────────────
Each side is free to spin at its own speed.  The wheel angular velocity on each
side is:

    ω_right  =  v_right / r                                                  (51)
    ω_left   =  v_left  / r                                                  (52)

Wheel rotation angle (accumulated for tread-stripe rendering):

    θ_right  ←  (θ_right  +  v_right · Δt / r)  mod  2π                   (53)
    θ_left   ←  (θ_left   +  v_left  · Δt / r)  mod  2π                   (54)

8.3  Locked differential
─────────────────────────
Both sides are constrained to the same rotational speed.  The common speed is
the average of the ideal individual speeds:

    v_avg  =  (v_right + v_left) / 2  =  v                                  (55)

    θ_right  ←  (θ_right  +  v_avg · Δt / r)  mod  2π                     (56)
    θ_left   ←  (θ_left   +  v_avg · Δt / r)  mod  2π                     (57)

Effect: during a turn the locked diff forces the inner tyre to spin faster than
its ideal speed and the outer tyre to spin slower, creating a net understeer
moment and tyre scrub.  This is visible in the viewport as both sides
displaying the average speed rather than differential speeds.

8.4  Passive (trailer) wheels
──────────────────────────────
Trailer axles are non-driven and non-steered.  A single shared rotation angle
is accumulated using the tractor's longitudinal speed as an approximation of
the trailer's forward speed:

    θ_trailer  ←  (θ_trailer  +  v · Δt / r)  mod  2π                     (58)

This is a visual approximation; no per-side differentiation is performed on
passive axles.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
9.  FIFTH-WHEEL KINEMATIC TRAILER MODEL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

9.1  System description
────────────────────────
An articulating trailer is coupled to the tractor via a fifth-wheel hitch at
position p_hitch along the tractor frame.  The hitch / kingpin is a pin joint
that constrains the trailer's front to follow the hitch point but allows free
rotation about the vertical axis (articulation angle φ = ψ_t − ψ).

State:  ψ_t  — trailer heading [rad],  same CW-from-North convention.

Parameters:
    L_t     — kingpin distance: hitch to trailer axle centroid [m]
    φ_max   — maximum articulation angle [rad]  (user-configurable)

9.2  Kinematic constraint
──────────────────────────
The trailer is modelled as a rigid body rolling on passive wheels without
lateral slip.  The no-lateral-slip constraint at the trailer axle centroid
means the trailer's velocity must be aligned with its heading.  This produces
the following first-order ODE for the trailer heading:

    dψ_t/dt  =  (v_hitch · sin(ψ − ψ_t)) / L_t                            (59)

where v_hitch ≈ v is the longitudinal speed of the tractor (equivalently the
speed of the hitch point in the direction of the tractor heading).

Derivation:  The hitch point moves at velocity v along the tractor's heading ψ.
The component of that velocity perpendicular to the trailer's heading ψ_t is
v · sin(ψ − ψ_t).  For the trailer to satisfy the no-slip constraint, the
axle must travel perpendicular to the trailer heading at the rate:

    v_perp  =  L_t · dψ_t/dt

Setting v_perp = v · sin(ψ − ψ_t) gives eq. (59).

Convergence: when ψ_t = ψ (trailer aligned with tractor) the right-hand side
is zero — the trailer heading is stable.  When ψ_t ≠ ψ the trailer is "pulled"
toward the tractor heading at a rate proportional to v and inversely
proportional to L_t.

9.3  Discrete-time update
──────────────────────────
Let Δψ = ψ − ψ_t be the heading difference, normalised to (−π, π]:

    Δψ_norm  =  (Δψ + π) mod 2π  −  π                                      (60)

First-order Euler step:
    ψ_t  ←  ψ_t  +  (v · sin(Δψ_norm) / L_t) · Δt                        (61)

9.4  Articulation angle clamping
──────────────────────────────────
After the kinematic update, the articulation angle φ = ψ_t − ψ is normalised
and hard-clamped:

    φ_norm  =  (φ + π) mod 2π  −  π                                        (62)
    φ_clamp =  clamp(φ_norm,  −φ_max,  +φ_max)                             (63)
    ψ_t     =  ψ + φ_clamp                                                  (64)

This prevents the trailer from jack-knifing beyond the mechanical stop.

9.5  Hitch screen position
───────────────────────────
In the vehicle-local painter frame (origin at CG, +y rearward), the hitch is
at longitudinal offset:

    h_local  =  (p_hitch − 0.5) · L_frame · PX_PER_M   [px]               (65)

After rotation by heading ψ, the hitch screen position is:

    h_x  =  cx  −  h_local · sin(ψ)                                        (66)
    h_y  =  cy  +  h_local · cos(ψ)                                        (67)

The trailer is then drawn in a coordinate frame translated to (h_x, h_y) and
rotated by ψ_t, with y = 0 at the hitch and y > 0 rearward along the trailer.

9.6  Trailing behaviour
────────────────────────
The kinematic model exhibits physically correct behaviour:

  Straight driving:   ψ_t → ψ  (trailer aligns with tractor)
  Steady-state turn:  φ_steady ≈ asin(v · Δt / (L_t · dψ/dt))
                               ≈ L_t_effective / R_tractor  for small angles
  Jack-knife:         prevented by φ_max clamp; analogous to articulation
                      stops on real semi-trucks (typically 45°–90°)


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10.  IDLE DETECTION AND EARLY EXIT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

To avoid numerical drift and unnecessary redraws when the vehicle is at rest,
the simulation applies a strict idle test:

    idle  ⟺  |v| < 10⁻⁴ m/s
             AND  |v_y| < 0.01 m/s
             AND  |ψ̇| < 0.01 rad/s
             AND  |τ| < 0.1 N·m                                             (68)

When idle, v_y and ψ̇ are zeroed and the tick exits early without integrating
positions or updating the trailer.  The physics timer still fires at 60 Hz but
does minimal work.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
11.  COMPLETE TICK SEQUENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Each Δt = 0.016 s the following steps execute in order:

  Step 1   _apply_drive_torque()
           τ_w = τ / n_drive                            [eq. 5]
           F_w = τ_w / r                                [eq. 6]
           N_w = m·g / n_total                          [eq. 7]
           w_fac = min(1.5, √(w_tyre / 0.20))          [eq. 8]
           F_lim = μ · N_w · w_fac                      [eq. 9]
           F_act = clamp(F_w, ±F_lim)                  [eq. 10]
           v ← v + (F_act · n_drive / m) · Δt         [eq. 12]
           v ← v · (1 − k_drag · Δt)                  [eq. 13]

  Step 2   Idle check                                   [eq. 68]
           → early return if idle

  Step 3   Slip angles
           δ_f, δ_r from steering mode                  [Section 4.2]
           α_f = sign(v)·δ_f − atan2(v_y + ψ̇·l_f, |v|) [eq. 15]
           α_r = sign(v)·δ_r − atan2(v_y − ψ̇·l_r, |v|) [eq. 16]

  Step 4   Normal loads
           N_f = m·g·l_r / L                            [eq. 17]
           N_r = m·g·l_f / L                            [eq. 18]

  Step 5   Cornering forces with saturation
           F_y,f = clamp(C_f·α_f, ±μ·N_f)             [eq. 19, 21]
           F_y,r = clamp(C_r·α_r, ±μ·N_r)             [eq. 20, 21]

  Step 6   Equations of motion (Euler integration)
           v_y ← v_y + [(F_y,f+F_y,r)/m − v·ψ̇] · Δt  [eq. 24]
           ψ̇  ← ψ̇  + [(l_f·F_y,f − l_r·F_y,r)/I_z] · Δt [eq. 25]

  Step 7   Lateral velocity clamp                       [eq. 30]
           |v_y| ≤ |v|

  Step 8   Low-speed fade                               [eq. 26-29]
           (applied when |v| < 2 m/s)

  Step 9   World position integration
           x_px ← x_px + (v·sin ψ − v_y·cos ψ) · PX/M · Δt  [eq. 39]
           y_px ← y_px + (−v·cos ψ − v_y·sin ψ) · PX/M · Δt [eq. 40]
           ψ    ← (ψ − ψ̇·Δt) mod 2π                         [eq. 41]

  Step 10  Differential wheel rotation
           Open:   θ_R += v_R·Δt/r,  θ_L += v_L·Δt/r        [eq. 53-54]
           Locked: θ_R = θ_L += v·Δt/r                        [eq. 56-57]

  Step 11  Fifth-wheel kinematic update  (if enabled)
           Δψ_norm = (ψ − ψ_t + π) mod 2π − π               [eq. 60]
           ψ_t ← ψ_t + (v·sin(Δψ_norm)/L_t)·Δt             [eq. 61]
           φ_clamp = clamp(ψ_t − ψ, ±φ_max)                 [eq. 63]
           ψ_t = ψ + φ_clamp                                  [eq. 64]
           θ_trailer += v·Δt/r                                [eq. 58]

  Step 12  Repaint viewport

12.  MODEL LIMITATIONS AND KNOWN APPROXIMATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

12.1  No longitudinal–lateral coupling
  The friction ellipse is not enforced across both directions simultaneously.
  Combined slip (e.g. braking while cornering) does not reduce the lateral
  friction limit.  The tyre forces are independently saturated.

12.2  Uniform mass and load
  Mass is fixed at 1 500 kg regardless of vehicle type.  Normal load does not
  transfer longitudinally under braking/acceleration or laterally in cornering.
  A full load-transfer model would use:
    ΔN_lateral  =  m · a_y · h_CG / T_track
    ΔN_longitudinal = m · a_x · h_CG / L

12.3  Single cornering stiffness
  C_f and C_r are constant.  Real tyres follow the Pacejka "Magic Formula"
  where stiffness varies with slip angle, normal load, and camber.  The
  saturation clamp in eq. (21) provides a rough approximation of the Pacejka
  peak without modelling the drop-off beyond peak slip.

12.4  Rigid tyre
  Tyre width affects only the longitudinal friction ceiling (eq. 8).  It does
  not affect cornering stiffness (which it should in a full model:
  C ∝ contact-patch length × lateral stiffness per unit length).

12.5  Linear-region bicycle model
  The bicycle model is valid for small slip angles (α < ~5–8°).  At larger
  slip angles the linear force law (eq. 19-20) overpredicts cornering force.
  The saturation (eq. 21) mitigates this but does not reproduce the rounded
  Pacejka shoulder.

12.6  Kinematic trailer (no inertia)
  The trailer uses a purely kinematic (massless, frictionless-pivot) model.
  In reality, the trailer has its own yaw inertia, which damps high-frequency
  articulation oscillations and delays jack-knife.

12.7  Symmetric cornering stiffness
  C_f = C_r = 60 000 N/rad gives K_us = 0 (neutral steer) for symmetric
  geometry.  Real vehicles are typically slightly understeering by design
  (K_us ≈ 0.002 – 0.005 rad·s²/m for passenger cars).
